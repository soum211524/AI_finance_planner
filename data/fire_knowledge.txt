## FIRE Planning Fundamentals

### What is FIRE?
FIRE stands for Financial Independence, Retire Early. It is a lifestyle movement focused on aggressive saving and investing to achieve financial independence earlier than traditional retirement age (60-65). There are variants: Lean FIRE (minimalist lifestyle), Fat FIRE (maintaining high lifestyle), Barista FIRE (semi-retirement with part-time work), and Coast FIRE (saving enough early that compound growth does the rest).

### The 25x Rule
The cornerstone of FIRE planning: you need a retirement corpus equal to 25 times your annual expenses. This is derived from the 4% Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR), proven by the Trinity Study. If annual expenses are ₹12,00,000, corpus needed = ₹3,00,00,000. The 4% rule means you withdraw 4% of your portfolio annually, adjusted for inflation, and historically this has lasted 30+ years with high probability.

### Safe Withdrawal Rate (SWR)
The 4% rule is a guideline. For Indian investors retiring early (30-40 years of retirement), a more conservative 3% to 3.5% SWR is often recommended. This means corpus = 28x to 33x annual expenses. Long retirement periods and sequence-of-returns risk justify this caution.

### Savings Rate and Time to FIRE
Savings rate is the single most powerful lever in FIRE planning:
- 10% savings rate: ~43 years to FIRE
- 20% savings rate: ~37 years to FIRE
- 30% savings rate: ~28 years to FIRE
- 40% savings rate: ~22 years to FIRE
- 50% savings rate: ~17 years to FIRE
- 60% savings rate: ~12 years to FIRE
- 70% savings rate: ~8.5 years to FIRE

### SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) Calculation
Monthly SIP required = FV * r / ((1+r)^n - 1)
Where FV = future value needed, r = monthly return rate, n = number of months.
For a target of ₹3 crore in 15 years at 12% annual return:
r = 12%/12 = 1% monthly, n = 180 months
Monthly SIP ≈ ₹40,000

### Asset Allocation by Age
The "100 minus age" rule for equity allocation is outdated. Modern approach:
- Age 20-30: 80-90% equity, 10-15% debt, 5-10% gold/REIT
- Age 30-40: 70-80% equity, 15-20% debt, 5-10% gold/REIT
- Age 40-50: 60-70% equity, 20-30% debt, 5-10% gold/REIT
- Age 50+ (pre-FIRE): 50-60% equity, 30-40% debt, 10% gold/REIT
- Post-FIRE: 40-50% equity, 40-50% debt, 10% gold/REIT

Equity provides growth to beat inflation. Debt provides stability and liquidity. Gold is a hedge against currency devaluation and geopolitical risk.

## Indian Investment Instruments

### Equity Mutual Funds
Best for long-term FIRE corpus building:
- Flexi-cap funds: Diversified across market cap, good for core portfolio
- Index funds (Nifty 50, Nifty Next 50): Low cost, market returns, FIRE-friendly
- Small-cap funds: Higher risk/reward, suitable for long horizon (10+ years)
- ELSS funds: Tax saving under Section 80C, 3-year lock-in, equity returns
Historical average returns: Large-cap 10-12% CAGR, Mid-cap 12-15% CAGR, Small-cap 14-18% CAGR over long periods.

### Debt Instruments
- PPF (Public Provident Fund): 7.1% tax-free, 15-year lock-in, Section 80C benefit, ₹1.5L annual limit
- EPF (Employee Provident Fund): 8.15% tax-free for most, mandatory for salaried, employer matches contribution
- NPS (National Pension System): 8-10% returns, additional ₹50K deduction under Section 80CCD(1B), partial withdrawal allowed
- Liquid funds: For emergency fund, better than savings account, 6-7% returns
- Short-duration bond funds: 6-8% returns, lower volatility than equity

### Gold Investment
- Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs): 2.5% annual interest + gold price appreciation, no capital gains tax on redemption, ideal for FIRE
- Gold ETFs: Liquid, low cost, tracks gold prices
- Target: 5-10% of portfolio in gold for hedging

### Real Estate (REITs)
- REITs provide real estate exposure without large capital commitment
- SEBI-regulated, mandatory 90% dividend distribution
- Returns: 7-9% yield + appreciation, good inflation hedge

## Tax Planning for FIRE

### Section 80C (₹1,50,000 limit)
Available deductions:
- ELSS mutual funds (best option for FIRE - combines tax saving + equity returns)
- PPF contribution
- EPF contribution
- Life insurance premium
- Principal repayment of home loan
- Tuition fees for children
- Tax saving FD (5-year)

### Section 80CCD(1B) - NPS (Additional ₹50,000)
Over and above 80C, invest ₹50,000 in NPS for additional tax benefit.
At 30% tax bracket: saves ₹15,000 in tax annually.
At 20% tax bracket: saves ₹10,000 in tax annually.

### Section 80D - Health Insurance
- Self and family: up to ₹25,000
- Parents (senior citizen): up to ₹50,000
- Total potential deduction: ₹75,000
- At 30% bracket: saves ₹22,500 annually

### LTCG Tax on Equity
As of 2024, equity mutual fund gains above ₹1,00,000 per year taxed at 10% LTCG.
Strategy: Book profits up to ₹1L each year to reset cost basis (tax harvesting).

### HRA Exemption
For salaried employees in rented accommodation: claim HRA exemption.
Exemption = minimum of (actual HRA received, rent paid - 10% of basic salary, 50% of basic for metro / 40% non-metro).

### Old vs New Tax Regime (FY 2024-25)
New regime is default but old regime often better if total deductions > ₹3.75L:
- Old regime with maximum deductions (80C + 80CCD + 80D + HRA): can save ₹3-4L in taxable income
- For income > ₹15L: calculate both and choose lower tax outgo

## Emergency Fund Planning

### Why 6 Months is the Standard
Emergency fund covers: job loss, medical emergencies, major repairs, family emergencies.
6 months of expenses is the minimum; 12 months recommended for self-employed or variable income earners.
Example: Monthly expenses ₹50,000 → Emergency fund = ₹3,00,000 to ₹6,00,000.

### Where to Keep Emergency Fund
- Liquid mutual funds: Better returns than savings account (6-7% vs 3-4%), withdraw within 1 business day
- High-yield savings account: Instant access, 4-7% interest
- Split approach: 3 months in savings account (instant), 3 months in liquid fund

### Building Emergency Fund First
Priority sequence for FIRE:
1. Build 3-month emergency fund first
2. Get term insurance and health insurance
3. Start SIPs for FIRE corpus
4. Max out 80C and 80CCD tax savings
5. Invest remaining surplus in additional SIPs

## Insurance Planning (Critical Gap in India)

### Term Life Insurance
Most Indians are severely underinsured. Rule of thumb: cover = 10-15x annual income.
For a ₹12L/year earner: ₹1.2 Cr to ₹1.8 Cr term cover.
Cost: ₹10,000-₹20,000/year for ₹1 Cr cover for a 30-year-old non-smoker.
Buy online pure term plans (no investment component, no surrender value).
Recommended: cover till age 60 or till FIRE age.

### Health Insurance
Employer health insurance is NOT sufficient for FIRE planning:
- Lapses on job change
- Insufficient cover (typically ₹3-5L)
Recommended personal health insurance: ₹10L-₹25L family floater
Cost: ₹15,000-₹35,000/year for ₹10L family floater (family of 3-4)
Add super top-up plan for additional cover at lower cost.

### Critical Illness Insurance
Covers cancer, heart attack, stroke, kidney failure, organ transplant etc.
Lump sum payout on diagnosis, not hospitalization reimbursement.
Cover: ₹25L-₹50L recommended.
Especially important if family history of critical illness.

### Common Insurance Gaps
- Traditional LIC endowment/money-back policies: Poor returns (4-6%), expensive, mixing insurance with investment → AVOID
- ULIP (Unit Linked Insurance Plans): High charges, mixed returns → AVOID
- Avoid endowment plans entirely; use pure term + mutual funds instead

## FIRE Milestone Planning

### Phase 1: Foundation (Years 1-3)
- Build 6-month emergency fund in liquid funds
- Get adequate term insurance (10-15x income)
- Get comprehensive health insurance (₹10L+ family floater)
- Start SIPs: ELSS (for 80C) + index fund/flexi-cap
- Max out 80C, 80CCD, 80D deductions
- Target savings rate: 30-40%

### Phase 2: Accumulation (Years 3-10)
- Step up SIPs 10-15% annually with salary increments
- Diversify into small-cap, international funds
- Build real estate exposure via REITs or physical property
- Annual portfolio review and rebalancing
- Target savings rate: 40-50%

### Phase 3: Growth (Years 10-20)
- Portfolio likely doing heavy lifting with compound growth
- Maximize NPS contributions for retirement phase
- Consider Sovereign Gold Bonds as corpus grows
- Start tax-harvesting LTCG annually
- Target savings rate: 50%+

### Phase 4: Pre-FIRE Glide Path (3-5 years before FIRE)
- Gradually shift equity to debt (glide path allocation)
- Build 2-3 year cash/near-cash buffer to avoid sequence of returns risk
- Review and confirm corpus adequacy
- Plan post-FIRE withdrawal strategy
- Consider healthcare costs inflation (medical inflation 10-15% vs regular 6%)

### The Coast FIRE Concept
If you have enough invested that compound growth alone will reach your target by traditional retirement age, you've achieved Coast FIRE. You no longer need to save aggressively; your investments will do the work. Coast FIRE number = Target corpus / (1 + rate)^years.

## Post-FIRE Withdrawal Strategy

### Bucket Strategy
Bucket 1: 1-2 years expenses in liquid/savings (₹12-24L for ₹12L/year expenses). No market risk.
Bucket 2: 3-7 years in short-term debt funds (₹36-84L). Refills Bucket 1.
Bucket 3: Remaining in equity. Refills Bucket 2 every 3-5 years.
This protects against sequence-of-returns risk.

### Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP)
Set up SWP from mutual fund portfolio for monthly income post-FIRE.
SWP of 3-4% annually from equity funds, remaining stays invested.
Tax efficient: only gains taxed (LTCG at 10%), not principal.

### Inflation Adjustment
4% SWR typically beats 6% inflation over long periods (equity returns ~12% > inflation 6% + SWR 4%).
Reassess annually: if markets fall >30%, consider reducing withdrawal temporarily.

## FIRE for Indian Context

### Special Considerations
- Family responsibilities: Joint family obligations, parents' healthcare, children's education typically higher in India
- Real estate: High proportion of net worth in real estate reduces liquidity; REITs are better for FIRE
- Gold: Cultural importance + financial hedge; 5-10% allocation reasonable
- Healthcare: Medical inflation 10-15%, buy health insurance early before pre-existing conditions
- Currency risk: Diversify 10-15% into international funds for rupee depreciation hedge

### Common Mistakes
1. Underestimating corpus needed (not accounting for healthcare, travel, hobbies post-FIRE)
2. Over-allocating to real estate (illiquid, high transaction costs)
3. Mixing insurance with investment (LIC endowment, ULIPs)
4. Not increasing SIPs with salary growth
5. Withdrawing SIPs during market downturns (biggest mistake)
6. Ignoring inflation in goal planning
7. Not having adequate term insurance (family financially exposed)
8. Under-insured on health (medical costs can derail FIRE)

### FIRE Numbers for Different Income Levels (India 2024)
Monthly expenses ₹30,000: FIRE corpus ₹90L, Monthly SIP needed ₹25,000 for 20 years
Monthly expenses ₹50,000: FIRE corpus ₹1.5 Cr, Monthly SIP needed ₹42,000 for 20 years
Monthly expenses ₹75,000: FIRE corpus ₹2.25 Cr, Monthly SIP needed ₹63,000 for 20 years
Monthly expenses ₹1,00,000: FIRE corpus ₹3 Cr, Monthly SIP needed ₹84,000 for 20 years
Monthly expenses ₹1,50,000: FIRE corpus ₹4.5 Cr, Monthly SIP needed ₹1,26,000 for 20 years
(Assumes 12% CAGR, 6% inflation adjustment, 4% SWR)
